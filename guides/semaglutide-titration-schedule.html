Semaglutide Titration Schedule: How Dose Escalation Actually Works

Whether you're on Ozempic, Wegovy, or compounded semaglutide, the titration logic is the same: start low, increase every four weeks, and let side effects — not the calendar alone — set the pace.

Every step is held for a minimum of four weeks. That's not caution for its own sake — it takes roughly four to five weeks of weekly dosing for semaglutide to reach steady state, the point where the amount entering your body each week equals the amount leaving. Judging a dose before steady state means judging it before it's fully arrived.

Three titration principles worth knowing

  1. The starter dose is for your gut, not the scale. 0.25 mg exists to train your digestive system. Don't be discouraged if week two looks unremarkable.
  2. Side effects gate the ladder. Persistent nausea, vomiting, or fatigue at a new step is a signal to hold, not push through silently. Prescribers routinely extend steps — a documented symptom log makes that conversation fast and factual.
  3. Higher isn't automatically better. The goal is the lowest dose that delivers results you can sustain. Some people maintain on 1.0 mg for years.

Why your titration history deserves a real log

Six months in, "what dose was I on in March, and how bad was the nausea?" is a question your prescriber will actually ask. Memory won't answer it. A log will.

Frequently asked questions

How long should you stay on each semaglutide dose?

The standard is a minimum of four weeks per step, which is how long semaglutide takes to approach steady state after a change. Your prescriber may extend a step if side effects are still active.

Can I titrate semaglutide faster if I feel fine?

Escalation pace is a prescriber decision. Even with no side effects, moving faster than four-week steps deviates from the label and increases risk. Bring your dose and symptom log to the conversation instead of self-adjusting.
